University of Canterbury Flight Club

I lead the drone side of the University of Canterbury Flight Club, helping coordinate weekly flying and maintaining the club's aircraft.

Every Friday, club members meet at Ilam Fields to fly fixed-wing aircraft and FPV drones, practise new skills and introduce new members to the hobby.

My responsibilities include maintaining the club drones, organising the drone activities during flight sessions, helping members with aircraft setup and troubleshooting, and supporting people who are learning to fly.

The role has helped me develop leadership and communication skills alongside my technical experience. It has also taught me how to explain unfamiliar systems clearly and help other people diagnose faults without simply taking over the task.

Flight Operations

Weekly flying sessions provide regular experience operating, inspecting and supporting a variety of small unmanned aircraft.

Before flying, the aircraft are checked for mechanical damage, loose components, battery condition and correct radio operation. During sessions, I help coordinate where and when aircraft are flown so that pilots can operate safely around each other.

This has reinforced the importance of pre-flight checks, communication, situational awareness and making conservative decisions when an aircraft is not operating correctly.

FPV Drone Experience

Alongside the Flight Club, I have developed practical experience building, configuring and flying FPV drones.

My first FPV aircraft was an EMAX Tinyhawk III Plus Freestyle, which provided a platform for learning Betaflight configuration, radio setup, tuning, repairs and FPV flight.

I later built a 3.5-inch Volador freestyle drone from individually selected components. The frame, motors, propellers, flight electronics, radio system, video system and battery setup were selected to achieve a balance of agility, durability, range and flight time.

Building the Volador required considering the full aircraft as a system rather than selecting each component independently. Propeller size, motor KV, battery voltage, frame mass and ESC capability all needed to be compatible with the intended flight performance.

Maintenance & Troubleshooting

Maintaining FPV aircraft has been just as valuable as building them. Frequent flying and occasional crashes create a wide range of mechanical, electrical and software faults that need to be diagnosed before the aircraft can safely return to service.

This work includes both my own aircraft and drones used through the Flight Club. Rather than replacing components without investigation, I try to isolate the root cause of each fault through inspection, continuity testing, configuration checks and controlled bench testing.

Examples of maintenance and troubleshooting work include:

Replacing failed ESCs
Replacing damaged motors
Soldering and repairing damaged boards
Repairing crash-damaged wiring and frames
Replacing damaged FPV antennas
Updating ExpressLRS firmware
Flashing Betaflight and INAV
Diagnosing receiver and channel issues
Reconfiguring and tuning aircraft after repairs

The most useful skill developed through this work has been systematic fault diagnosis. UAV faults often appear as symptoms such as unstable flight, video interference, receiver dropouts or inconsistent motor behaviour, so each subsystem must be checked methodically until the problem can be isolated.
