Project Overview

This project explores the design of a fixed-wing UAV where endurance and useful mission capability are the primary goals.

Unlike my VTOL aircraft, this design does not need to support itself using vertical thrust. This allows the aircraft to be developed around efficient wing-borne flight, where the propulsion system only needs to overcome aerodynamic drag.

The aircraft is being designed around a payload rather than treating payload capacity as an afterthought. This requires the aerodynamic layout, internal volume, centre of gravity and structural design to be considered as one integrated system.

The long-term goal is to create a practical autonomous platform capable of remaining airborne for extended periods while carrying sensors or other mission equipment.

Aircraft & CAD Design

The aircraft was developed in CAD to balance aerodynamic efficiency, structural requirements, payload volume and manufacturability.

The long, slender wing provides a higher aspect ratio than my smaller aircraft, reducing induced drag and improving efficiency during cruise. The fuselage provides space for batteries, avionics and payload equipment while maintaining a streamlined outer shape.

Particular attention was given to centre-of-gravity control. Batteries and payloads may change between missions, so the internal layout needs to allow their positions to be adjusted without requiring a major redesign.

The CAD model also allows different components and configurations to be evaluated before committing to manufacturing.

Overall Layout

The aircraft uses a conventional fixed-wing configuration to keep the aerodynamic and control system relatively simple while maximising efficiency.

A long wingspan provides the lifting area required for low wing loading and efficient cruise. The tail surfaces are positioned far behind the wing, providing a useful moment arm for pitch and yaw stability without requiring excessively large control surfaces.

Keeping the layout conventional also reduces development risk. This allows more attention to be placed on the propulsion system, structural efficiency, autonomous operation and payload integration.

Designing for Endurance

Endurance is not determined by one component. It depends on the interaction between aerodynamic drag, aircraft mass, propulsion efficiency, battery capacity and operating speed.

Increasing battery capacity may increase the available energy, but it also increases mass and the power required to keep the aircraft airborne. Similarly, a larger wing may reduce wing loading but also adds structural weight and surface drag.

The design therefore requires a balance between several competing factors:

Aerodynamic Efficiency

Reducing drag and selecting an efficient cruise condition lowers the power required to maintain flight.

Structural Mass

The airframe must remain sufficiently stiff and durable without consuming unnecessary payload or battery capacity.

Propulsion Matching

Motor, propeller and battery choices must be matched to the expected cruise speed rather than selected only for maximum static thrust.

Payload Integration

Payload size, mass and placement influence stability, centre of gravity and the overall aerodynamic shape.

Design principle

The goal is not simply to install the largest possible battery. The aircraft must minimise the power required to fly so that each unit of stored energy produces the greatest useful endurance.

Autonomous Flight

The aircraft is intended to operate as an autonomous platform rather than only as a manually controlled RC aircraft.

Autonomous operation would allow the aircraft to follow waypoint missions, maintain altitude and airspeed, return to its launch point and carry out repeatable flight paths for sensing or surveying tasks.

Reliable autonomous flight depends on more than software. The aircraft must first have predictable stability, adequate control authority and dependable propulsion and sensor systems.

Development will therefore progress from manual flight testing through assisted flight modes before introducing longer autonomous missions.

Development Approach

This project is being developed incrementally, with each stage intended to reduce risk before moving to more complex testing.

1. Aircraft Design

Establish the aerodynamic layout, structural concept, internal packaging and approximate centre-of-gravity range.

2. Prototype & Manufacture

Produce components, validate assembly methods and identify practical manufacturing limitations.

3. Manual Flight Testing

Confirm basic stability, control response, stall behaviour and structural performance.

4. Autonomous Integration

Introduce assisted modes, waypoint navigation and longer mission testing once the aircraft is mechanically reliable.

This staged process makes faults easier to isolate and avoids attempting to solve aerodynamic, structural, electronic and autonomy problems at the same time.

Connection to My Other UAV Work

This project complements the VTOL UAV by exploring the opposite side of the design problem.

The VTOL project prioritises flexible takeoff and landing while managing the complexity of hover and transition flight. The endurance aircraft removes that vertical-flight requirement, allowing more focus to be placed on aerodynamic efficiency, payload integration and long-range autonomous operation.

Knowledge developed through my earlier fixed-wing builds and VTOL testing has influenced the approach to stability, structure, manufacturing and field testing used in this project.

Key Lessons

This project has reinforced that endurance is a system-level design problem. Aerodynamics, propulsion, energy storage, payload and structure cannot be optimised independently.

It has also highlighted the importance of designing around the intended mission. A useful UAV must do more than fly for a long time; it must carry the required equipment, remain stable as its loading changes and operate reliably through repeated autonomous missions.

The project continues to build my experience in aircraft design, CAD, aerodynamic decision-making, autonomous systems and practical field testing.
