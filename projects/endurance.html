Long Endurance UAV

A fixed-wing UAV project focused on efficient aircraft design, lightweight 3D printed construction, aerodynamic performance, and payload-capable autonomous flight.

Project Overview

This project was developed as a fixed-wing UAV platform focused on efficiency, endurance, and practical autonomous flight capability. Unlike the VTOL aircraft, this design was based around conventional fixed-wing performance, with the goal of maximising aerodynamic efficiency and useful payload capacity.

The aircraft was also used as a development step toward more complex UAV projects. Before committing to the additional complexity of VTOL transition flight, this platform allowed me to explore efficient 3D printed aircraft design, structural layout, aerodynamic behaviour, and real flight testing in a simpler fixed-wing configuration.

Long endurance fixed-wing UAV platform.

Design Goals

The main aim of the aircraft was to create a practical fixed-wing UAV that could fly efficiently while still being simple enough to manufacture, repair, and iterate using 3D printing.

Primary Goal Efficient fixed-wing UAV development
Manufacturing 3D printed airframe components
Focus Endurance, stability and payload capability
Development Role Test platform for future autonomous UAV design

Airframe & CAD Design

The airframe was designed around efficient fixed-wing flight rather than vertical lift. This allowed more design focus to be placed on wing geometry, fuselage packaging, structural efficiency, and low-drag layout.

A key part of the design process was learning how to create a 3D printed aircraft that could remain lightweight while still being strong enough for real flight loads. This involved considering print orientation, wall thickness, internal structure, spar placement, and how loads would pass through the wing and fuselage.

This project became an important stepping stone for later UAV work because it helped validate the manufacturing approach before applying similar methods to more complex VTOL aircraft.

Aerodynamic Development

The aerodynamic design was focused on producing stable and efficient forward flight. The aircraft needed enough wing area for manageable takeoff and landing speeds while still maintaining a clean layout for efficient cruise.

Important considerations included wing loading, tail sizing, centre of gravity location, stall behaviour, and overall stability. These decisions directly affect how easy the aircraft is to launch, control, and recover during testing.

One of the major lessons from earlier fixed-wing testing was the importance of low-speed stall behaviour. After experiencing a tip stall issue on an earlier aircraft, I placed more emphasis on predictable stall characteristics and control authority at low speed.

Structural Design & 3D Printing

Because the aircraft was 3D printed, structural design had to be considered from the beginning. The challenge was not simply making parts strong, but making them strong enough while keeping the overall aircraft mass low.

In aircraft design, excess mass quickly affects takeoff performance, stall speed, endurance and payload capability. For this reason, increasing infill or wall thickness everywhere was not an ideal solution.

Instead, the design process focused on placing material where it contributed most to stiffness and strength. This meant thinking carefully about internal structure, print direction, spar integration and the load paths between the wing, fuselage and tail.

Key Challenge: Strength Without Excess Weight

The most important structural challenge was achieving enough stiffness for reliable flight while keeping the aircraft light. The project helped develop an understanding of how 3D printed parts behave under real flight loads and how to design printed components around those limitations.

Flight Testing & Iteration

Flight testing was used to validate the assumptions made during CAD design and construction. This stage was essential because real aircraft behaviour often reveals issues that are difficult to fully predict during the design phase.

Testing provided feedback on stability, control response, structural stiffness, launch behaviour, and general handling. That feedback was then used to guide future design changes and improve later aircraft.

What This Aircraft Taught Me

This project helped me understand the full development loop of a UAV: design, manufacture, test, analyse and improve. It also highlighted how important it is to design with repairability and iteration in mind, especially when developing experimental aircraft.

Connection to the VTOL Project

This aircraft played an important role in the wider development of my UAV work. Before attempting a full VTOL aircraft, I wanted to better understand how to design, manufacture and fly a 3D printed fixed-wing UAV successfully.

The lessons from this fixed-wing platform directly influenced the later VTOL design, especially in areas such as wing structure, print orientation, aerodynamic layout, centre of gravity placement and flight testing strategy.

In that sense, this project was not only a standalone UAV, but also a testbed that helped reduce risk before moving on to a more complex transition-capable aircraft.

Key Lessons

The main lesson from this project was that efficient aircraft design depends on the interaction between aerodynamics, structure, weight and manufacturability. A design that looks good in CAD still needs to be practical to build, strong enough to fly, and stable enough to test safely.

The project also reinforced the value of incremental development. By first building and testing a more conventional fixed-wing UAV, I was able to develop skills and design confidence that became useful when working on the more complex VTOL platform.
