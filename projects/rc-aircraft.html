Where It Started

Every engineering project has a starting point. Mine began at 11 years old with a second-hand RC aircraft and a fascination with anything that could fly.

Receiving my first aircraft was the beginning of a long-term interest in aviation. Learning to fly involved plenty of crashes, repairs and rebuilding, but it also introduced me to the basics of aircraft control, centre of gravity and practical maintenance.

More importantly, it made me want to understand how aircraft were built rather than simply learning how to operate them.

Age 11 with my first RC aircraft, a second-hand foamboard Cub.

Age 12 with the first RC aircraft I built myself.

My First Scratch Build

Not long after learning to fly, I built my first aircraft from foamboard. This changed the hobby from something I participated in into something I could design, construct and modify myself.

The build introduced me to practical aircraft construction, control-surface installation, motor and servo mounting, wiring, weight distribution and centre-of-gravity placement.

It also taught me that even simple aircraft require careful decisions. Small changes to weight, stiffness or control throw could significantly change how the aircraft behaved in the air.

Building More Ambitious Aircraft

As my confidence improved, I began building aircraft with more demanding shapes and flight characteristics.

The Mustang was a step toward more scale-inspired foamboard aircraft. Compared with my earlier builds, it required cleaner construction, better alignment and more careful attention to weight and balance.

Each new aircraft became an opportunity to improve construction quality and understand why different layouts produced different handling characteristics.

Foamboard Mustang built as I progressed toward more complex and scale-inspired aircraft.

Edge Aerobatic Aircraft

The Edge is my most recent foamboard aircraft and represents how far my building and flying skills have progressed from the early projects.

The aircraft was built for responsive aerobatic flight and has considerably greater control authority than my earlier foamboard aircraft.

Flying it required more confidence and precision, especially when performing manoeuvres such as rolls and recovering from unusual attitudes.

The Edge has been one of the most enjoyable aircraft I have built because it combines the satisfaction of constructing the airframe with the challenge of learning to fly it well.

Early 3D-Printed Aircraft Experiment

Wanting to experiment with additive manufacturing, I attempted to build an entirely 3D-printed aircraft.

The aircraft never achieved successful flight. At the time, I had limited experience designing lightweight printed structures, and the printer available to me could not produce the thin, consistent parts required for an efficient airframe.

The result was simply too heavy for its available wing area and propulsion system.

Although unsuccessful, the project became a valuable early lesson in the limitations of FDM printing, weight management and stiffness-to-weight ratio. These lessons later influenced the structural approach used in my VTOL UAV, where custom internal reinforcement and print orientation became major design considerations.

Why This Matters to Me

These aircraft were much simpler than my current UAV projects, but they established the practical understanding I now rely on when designing more advanced systems.

They taught me that aircraft development is iterative, crashes are valuable feedback, and a design only becomes meaningful once it has been built and flown.

More than anything, they show that my interest in aviation did not begin with a university project. It has been something I have pursued for as long as I can remember, and it continues to shape the engineering work I choose to take on.
