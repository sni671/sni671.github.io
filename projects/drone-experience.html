University of Canterbury Flight Club

I lead the drone side of the University of Canterbury Flight Club, helping coordinate weekly flying and maintain the club's UAVs.

Every Friday, club members meet at Ilam Fields to fly fixed-wing aircraft and quadcopters, practise new skills and introduce new members to RC aviation.

My responsibilities include maintaining the club drones, coordinating drone operations during flying sessions, helping members configure and troubleshoot their aircraft, and supporting newer pilots as they learn.

The role has developed my leadership, mentoring and communication skills alongside my technical knowledge. It has also taught me how to explain unfamiliar systems clearly and guide others through a troubleshooting process.

Flight Operations

Weekly flying sessions provide regular experience operating, inspecting and supporting a range of small unmanned aircraft.

Before flying, aircraft are checked for mechanical damage, loose components, battery condition and correct radio operation. During sessions, I help coordinate aircraft movements so pilots can operate safely around one another.

This experience has reinforced the importance of pre-flight checks, clear communication, situational awareness and making conservative decisions whenever an aircraft is not operating correctly.

Flight Club Autonomous UAV Project

I am also involved in a Flight Club project developing a larger electric autonomous UAV as a practical platform for engineering, experimentation and student learning.

The aircraft is being designed for applications such as search and rescue, surveying, conservation, crop and livestock management, atmospheric monitoring and emergency supply delivery.

The current project targets include a maximum all-up mass below 14.5 kg, at least 60 minutes of flight time at maximum mass and a minimum horizontal range of 25 km. Major subassemblies are intended to be removable for maintenance, design iteration and transport in a standard car.

The future intention is for the aircraft to operate autonomously using ArduPilot, supported by onboard airspeed, altitude, position, traffic and inertial sensing.

The project provides a platform for members to contribute to aircraft design, CAD, analysis, embedded systems and flight testing.

Propulsion Test Rig

Part of the current development work involves preparing a wind-tunnel propulsion test rig. The proposed system uses an ESC and motor mounted inside the tunnel, with power and signal cables routed externally.

The test setup is being developed to control discrete throttle levels while logging voltage and current. This data can then be combined with load-cell and airspeed measurements to evaluate thrust across a range of throttle settings and wind-tunnel velocities.

An ESP32-based controller is being considered to provide PWM motor control, electrical measurements, data logging and a simple laptop interface without requiring an additional flight-controller software layer.

FPV Quad Building

Alongside the Flight Club, I have developed practical experience building, configuring and flying FPV quadcopters.

My first dedicated FPV platform was an EMAX Tinyhawk III Plus Freestyle, which allowed me to learn Betaflight configuration, radio setup, tuning, maintenance and FPV flight.

I later built a 3.5-inch Volador freestyle quad from individually selected components. The frame, motors, propellers, electronics, radio system, video system and battery setup were selected to balance agility, durability, transmission range and flight time.

Component selection required considering the quad as a complete system. Motor KV, propeller size, battery voltage, frame mass and ESC capability all influenced the final flight behaviour.

Maintenance & Troubleshooting

Maintaining quadcopters has been just as valuable as building them. Frequent flying and occasional crashes create mechanical, electrical and software faults that must be diagnosed before an aircraft can safely return to service.

This work includes both my own aircraft and drones used through the Flight Club. Rather than replacing components without investigation, I try to isolate each problem through inspection, continuity testing, configuration checks and controlled bench testing.

The most valuable skill developed through this work has been systematic fault diagnosis. UAV faults often appear as symptoms such as unstable flight, video interference, receiver dropouts or inconsistent motor behaviour, so each subsystem must be checked methodically until the underlying problem is isolated.
