VTOL UAV

A 3D printed tricopter-style VTOL UAV developed to explore vertical takeoff, transition flight, and efficient fixed-wing operation through real-world testing and iterative engineering design.

Watch Flight Test

Project Overview

This project began as an attempt to combine two very different aircraft behaviours: the ability to take off and land vertically, and the efficiency of fixed-wing forward flight. The result is a tricopter-style VTOL aircraft with two front tilt motors and a rear propulsion motor.

The aircraft was designed, manufactured, wired, configured and tested as a complete system. The project involved CAD design, CFD analysis, structural design, 3D printing, electronics integration, flight controller configuration, vertical flight testing and forward flight development.

A major part of the project was not just building the final aircraft, but working through the failures and design changes required to make the platform more reliable. Several early design choices had to be revised after real flight testing exposed issues that were not obvious in CAD or simulation.

CAD Design

The aircraft was designed in CAD before manufacturing. This allowed the major components to be packaged together before committing to physical parts. The CAD model was used to position the motors, wing spar, tail boom, battery, electronics bay, control surfaces and fuselage structure.

The main structural layout uses a 10 × 8 × 500 mm carbon fibre wing spar and a 16 × 14 × 500 mm carbon fibre tail boom. These carbon fibre members provide stiffness while allowing the 3D printed parts to be used mainly for geometry, mounting, packaging and load transfer.

CAD model used to develop the aircraft layout, structure and component packaging.

One of the key CAD challenges was designing the aircraft so that it could actually be built and modified easily. The aircraft needed to be strong enough for flight, but also modular enough that individual parts could be reprinted and replaced during development.

Structural Design & 3D Printing

A major challenge in this project was designing printed parts that were both strong and lightweight. Simply increasing wall thickness or using high infill would make the parts stronger, but it would also increase mass quickly and reduce flight performance.

To solve this, many of the aircraft components were designed with custom internal structures rather than relying only on default slicer infill. This allowed material to be placed where it was useful for load paths, while keeping low-stress regions lighter.

This approach was particularly important for the fuselage, motor mounting regions and wing support structure. The goal was to make the printed parts behave more like engineered aircraft components rather than solid plastic blocks.

Aerodynamics & CFD

CFD was used as a design aid to understand the general aerodynamic behaviour of the aircraft. The goal was not to perfectly predict the full flight envelope, but to identify trends in the flow field and support design decisions around the wing and fuselage geometry.

The analysis helped visualise pressure regions and flow behaviour across the aircraft. This was especially useful when considering stability, wing loading and low-speed behaviour.

CFD pressure visualisation used to support aerodynamic development.

One of the most important aerodynamic lessons came from flight testing rather than simulation. An early version of the aircraft experienced a low-speed tip stall, causing the aircraft to roll off and crash. After reviewing the behaviour, I modified the wing design to include washout, reducing the angle of attack at the wing tips relative to the root.

This change was intended to make the root stall before the tips, improving low-speed handling and making the stall behaviour more predictable.

Electronics Architecture

The electronics system was built around a Corewing F405 flight controller running INAV. The aircraft uses a combination of propulsion, servo actuation, navigation sensors and video transmission to support manual testing and future autonomous operation.

Development Challenges & Iterations

The most important part of this project was the iteration process. Several problems only appeared during physical testing, and each one required analysis, redesign and retesting.

Challenge 1: Low-Speed Tip Stall

An early version of the aircraft crashed after entering a low-speed tip stall. The aircraft lost roll stability when the wing tips stalled before the root, causing a rapid roll-off.

To address this, I redesigned the wing to include washout. By reducing the effective angle of attack towards the wing tips, the aircraft should maintain better aileron effectiveness and produce a more controlled stall progression.

This was one of the clearest examples of flight testing directly changing the design.

Challenge 2: Motor Mount Deformation

During vertical flight testing, the original PLA motor mounting plates began to deform slightly. The issue was caused by heat from the motors softening the surrounding plastic during sustained hover testing.

As the material softened, the mounting screws began to pull through the plates. Although the parts were strong enough under static loading, the combination of heat, vibration and motor loads exposed a weakness in the design.

The motor mounting system was then redesigned to better distribute load and improve resistance to thermal deformation.

Challenge 3: Strength vs Weight

A recurring challenge throughout the project was balancing strength and weight. In small aircraft, extra mass quickly affects thrust margin, stall speed and endurance. However, parts still need to survive vibration, landing loads and flight stresses.

Instead of relying only on default slicer infill, I designed custom internal structures into many parts. This gave better control over the load paths and allowed high-stress areas to be reinforced without making the entire component unnecessarily heavy.

Vertical Flight

Vertical flight testing focused on validating the propulsion system, thrust margin, control response and structural stiffness. Hover is demanding because the aircraft is fully dependent on active control rather than aerodynamic stability from the wing.

This stage also revealed practical integration issues, including vibration, motor heating, servo loading and the importance of reliable mechanical mounting.

Transition Flight

Transition flight is the most technically challenging part of the project. During transition, the aircraft moves from rotor-borne flight to wing-borne flight. At the same time, the front motors rotate forward, airspeed increases, aerodynamic lift builds and the control surfaces become more effective.

This creates a complex control problem because the aircraft changes behaviour continuously throughout the transition. The goal is to manage this change smoothly without losing pitch stability, airspeed or control authority.

Forward Flight

In forward flight, the aircraft operates more like a conventional fixed-wing UAV. The wing generates most of the lift and the propulsion system provides forward thrust. This is the mode intended to provide the efficiency advantage over a multicopter-style aircraft.

Forward flight testing is used to evaluate stability, handling, cruise efficiency and control surface effectiveness. It also provides feedback on whether the aerodynamic and structural design decisions made earlier in the project are working as intended.

Key Lessons

The biggest lesson from this project is that aircraft development is iterative. CAD, CFD and calculations are valuable tools, but they do not replace physical testing. Real flight testing exposed issues with stall behaviour, thermal deformation, structural stiffness and system integration that were not obvious from the digital model alone.

This project has developed my skills in aircraft design, CAD, aerodynamics, 3D printing, electronics integration, flight control systems and practical debugging. More importantly, it has shown me how important it is to respond to test results with clear engineering changes rather than treating failures as dead ends.
