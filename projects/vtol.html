Structural Design & Additive Manufacturing

One of the largest engineering challenges throughout this project was developing an airframe that was sufficiently strong for flight while remaining lightweight enough to achieve practical flight performance.

Although additive manufacturing offers enormous design freedom, FDM printing is often overlooked for aircraft because standard thermoplastics generally provide a lower stiffness-to-weight ratio than traditional composite construction. Many modern printed aircraft overcome this limitation through lightweight foaming filaments or composite structures such as carbon fibre or fibreglass. At the time of this project, however, I did not have access to these materials or the resources required for composite manufacturing.

Instead, I challenged myself to achieve the highest possible structural efficiency using conventional PLA through careful engineering design.

Unlike conventional engineering structures, FDM printed parts are inherently anisotropic, meaning their mechanical properties depend heavily on print orientation. This made print orientation a design parameter that was considered alongside the geometry of every structural component. Layer direction, expected loading, printability and manufacturing constraints all influenced the final design.

Rather than relying on automatically generated slicer infill, every major structural component of the aircraft was designed with custom internal reinforcement. This provided complete control over where material was placed, allowing high-stress regions to be reinforced while reducing unnecessary mass elsewhere.

The objective was to produce printed parts that behaved as engineered structural components rather than simply solid plastic parts.

Fuselage Structure

The fuselage was designed around a continuous helical rib structure running the length of the aircraft.

Rather than filling the fuselage with conventional slicer infill, these ribs were designed specifically around the expected loading conditions during flight. The helical arrangement significantly improves both bending stiffness and torsional rigidity while using considerably less material than a solid structure.

The internal geometry also improves load transfer between the wing attachment, battery compartment and tail boom while maintaining sufficient space for electronics, wiring and maintenance access.

Custom-designed helical reinforcement ribs used throughout the fuselage to maximise stiffness while minimising weight.

Because the fuselage was designed as a modular assembly, the connection between the front and rear sections naturally became one of the highest loaded regions of the aircraft.

To prevent this joint becoming a structural weak point, 1.2 mm steel locating pins were incorporated into the design. These pins transfer bending loads across the joint while maintaining accurate alignment between the fuselage sections, significantly improving both strength and durability.

Wing Structure

The wings presented a different engineering challenge.

Due to the size limitations of the printer, each wing had to be manufactured as four individual printed sections, excluding the aileron.

Following research into lightweight aircraft structures and internal wing reinforcement methods, the internal geometry was designed around a series of diagonal reinforcing ribs. This arrangement provides excellent stiffness while efficiently distributing aerodynamic loads throughout the wing.

Like the fuselage, the wing joints also use 1.2 mm steel locating pins to improve alignment while transferring structural loads between adjacent printed sections.

Design Iteration

One of the most valuable aspects of this project has been the iterative design process.

Every redesign improved something—whether that was weight, stiffness, manufacturability, assembly, print time or reliability. Rather than viewing failed components as wasted effort, each one represented a better understanding of how the aircraft behaved and how the design could be improved.

This iterative approach ultimately led to a significantly stronger, lighter and more maintainable airframe than would have been possible by attempting to perfect the design before manufacturing.
