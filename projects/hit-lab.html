HIT Lab Mixed Reality Project

A research internship project involving mechanical design, 3D printed prototyping, Unity development, and mixed reality tracking.

Project Overview

During my time at the Human Interface Technology Lab, I worked on the design and development of a physical latching button mechanism intended for mixed reality interaction.

The project combined mechanical prototyping with Unity development. My work involved researching existing latching mechanisms, designing and 3D printing my own mechanism, learning Unity basics, and beginning to track the physical design inside a mixed reality environment.

The project gave me experience working in a research environment where the goal was not only to build a final object, but to explore how physical mechanisms could be connected with digital interaction.

Why I Chose HIT Lab

I was interested in HIT Lab because it offered the chance to work around innovative technologies such as mixed reality, while also developing hands-on prototyping and testing skills.

The project also gave me an opportunity to improve my coding ability, gain exposure to postgraduate research, and work in a collaborative lab environment.

Research Phase

The first stage of the project involved researching existing latching and push-push button mechanisms. I looked at how different mechanisms achieved state changes, how they could be manufactured, and which designs were realistic to prototype using 3D printing.

This stage helped me understand the constraints of the problem before starting my own design. I used Miro to organise ideas, compare mechanisms, and decide which design direction was most practical.

Research phase showing existing mechanisms, printed examples, and early design thinking.

Mechanical Design Development

The initial mechanism was designed in Onshape. This allowed me to quickly create and test the basic geometry of the button and understand how the moving parts would interact.

After the first design stage, I moved the model into Autodesk Fusion to develop it more robustly using a parametric design workflow. This made it easier to adjust dimensions, tolerances, clearances, and spring positions as the design evolved.

One of the key improvements was changing the mechanism into a more cylindrical shape. This made it more ergonomic to hold and reduced the effect of gravity and orientation on the mechanism.

Springs & Mechanical Behaviour

The spring system was an important part of the mechanism because it controlled how the button moved, returned, and latched between states.

This required testing the interaction between printed geometry, spring force, friction, and clearances. Small changes in tolerance had a noticeable effect on whether the mechanism moved smoothly or bound up.

Spring and mechanism development used to refine the button feel and latching behaviour.

Prototype Iteration

The design was developed using an iterative prototyping process. Each printed version revealed something new about the mechanism, such as where tolerances were too tight, where parts needed more support, or where the user interaction could be improved.

Challenge: Designing for 3D Printing

A major challenge was designing a mechanism that worked reliably when manufactured on a 3D printer. Clearances that appeared suitable in CAD did not always behave the same once printed, especially where friction, layer lines and small surface imperfections affected the movement.

Challenge: Mechanism Feel

The mechanism needed to be functional, but also comfortable and intuitive to use. This meant the shape, travel, spring force and latching behaviour all had to be considered together rather than treated as separate problems.

Unity Development

A major part of the project was learning Unity and using it to connect the physical mechanism with a digital mixed reality environment.

This involved learning the Unity hierarchy, writing scripts in C#, using prefabs, importing and working with the Meta SDK, setting up player movement and camera behaviour, and experimenting with animations.

Unity development work used to begin connecting the physical mechanism to a mixed reality environment.

Mixed Reality Tracking

Once the mechanism had been designed and printed, the next stage was tracking it inside Unity. The goal was to create a connection between a physical object and its digital representation.

This introduced a different set of challenges compared with mechanical design. Instead of only thinking about geometry and movement, I also had to consider how the object would be recognised, tracked, and represented in a mixed reality environment.

Tracking work used to detect and represent the physical mechanism in Unity.

Results

By the end of the project, I had researched existing latching mechanisms, designed and printed my own mechanism, developed a more ergonomic cylindrical design, learned core Unity workflows, and begun integrating the physical prototype with mixed reality tracking.

Final prototype and results from the HIT Lab project.

Future Development

The next steps for the project would be to improve the tracking method, fix remaining Unity-side bugs, and add code that allows Unity to detect the physical state of the mechanism.

Further mechanical development could also involve experimenting with different push-push mechanism designs and making the tracking system more subtle so that the object feels more natural to use.

Key Lessons

This project helped me develop a broader understanding of mechatronics beyond traditional robotics or UAV work. It required mechanical design, 3D printing, user interaction thinking, software development, and mixed reality integration.

The biggest lesson was that physical-digital interaction requires both good mechanical design and good software integration. A mechanism can work physically, but still need careful consideration before it works well as part of an interactive mixed reality system.
