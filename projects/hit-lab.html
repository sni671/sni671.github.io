HIT Lab Mixed Reality Project

A research internship project involving mechanical design, 3D printed prototyping, Unity development, and mixed reality tracking.

Project Overview

During my time at the Human Interface Technology Lab, I worked on the design and development of a physical latching button mechanism intended for mixed reality interaction.

The project combined mechanical prototyping with Unity development. My work involved researching existing latching mechanisms, designing and 3D printing my own mechanism, learning Unity basics, and beginning to track the physical design inside a mixed reality environment.

Final 3D printed button prototypes developed during the project.

Research Phase

The first stage involved researching existing latching and push-push button mechanisms. I looked at how different mechanisms achieved state changes, how they could be manufactured, and which designs were realistic to prototype using 3D printing.

This phase helped define the design direction before starting my own mechanism. I used research, printed examples, and idea mapping to decide which approach was most practical.

Research and idea development for possible latching mechanism designs.

Mechanical Design Development

The initial mechanism was designed in Onshape to quickly test the basic geometry and understand how the moving parts would interact. After this, I moved to Autodesk Fusion to develop the design more robustly using a parametric workflow.

Using a parametric model made it easier to adjust clearances, tolerances, spring positions, and part dimensions as the design evolved.

One of the key design improvements was moving toward a cylindrical form. This made the mechanism more ergonomic to hold and reduced the effect of gravity and orientation on the mechanism behaviour.

Spring Mechanism

The spring system was a critical part of the mechanism. It controlled how the button moved, returned, and latched between states.

This required testing the interaction between printed geometry, spring force, friction, and clearances. Small changes in tolerance had a noticeable effect on whether the mechanism moved smoothly or bound up.

Spring mechanism development and internal button behaviour.

Prototype Iteration

The design was developed through repeated 3D printing, assembly and testing. Each version revealed new issues with tolerances, friction, ergonomics or printability.

Challenge: Designing for 3D Printing

Clearances that looked suitable in CAD did not always behave the same once printed. Layer lines, surface finish, friction and small dimensional errors all affected the movement of the mechanism.

Challenge: Mechanism Feel

The mechanism needed to be functional, but also comfortable and intuitive to use. This meant the shape, travel, spring force and latching behaviour had to be considered together.

Unity Development

A major part of the project was learning Unity and using it to connect the physical mechanism with a digital mixed reality environment.

This involved learning the Unity hierarchy, writing scripts in C#, using prefabs, importing and working with the Meta SDK, setting up player movement and camera behaviour, and experimenting with animations.

Unity work used to begin connecting the physical mechanism to a mixed reality environment.

Mixed Reality Tracking

Once the mechanism had been designed and printed, the next stage was tracking it inside Unity. The goal was to create a connection between a physical object and its digital representation.

This introduced a different set of challenges compared with mechanical design. I had to consider how the object would be recognised, tracked, and represented in a mixed reality environment.

Tracking test for representing the physical mechanism inside Unity.

Results & Future Development

By the end of the project, I had researched existing latching mechanisms, designed and printed my own mechanism, developed a more ergonomic cylindrical design, learned core Unity workflows, and begun integrating the physical prototype with mixed reality tracking.

Future development would involve improving the tracking method, fixing remaining Unity-side bugs, and adding code that allows Unity to detect the physical state of the mechanism.

Key Lessons

This project helped me develop a broader understanding of mechatronics beyond traditional robotics or UAV work. It required mechanical design, 3D printing, user interaction thinking, software development, and mixed reality integration.

The biggest lesson was that physical-digital interaction requires both good mechanical design and good software integration. A mechanism can work physically, but still needs careful consideration before it works well as part of an interactive mixed reality system.
