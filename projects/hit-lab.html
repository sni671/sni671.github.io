Project Overview

During my time at the Human Interface Technology Laboratory, I worked on developing a physical button mechanism intended for interaction within a mixed reality environment.

The project combined mechanical design and digital interaction. My work involved researching existing push-push and latching mechanisms, developing a custom 3D-printed design, learning Unity and beginning to track the physical prototype within a mixed reality scene.

Unlike a conventional switch, the mechanism needed to provide clear physical movement while also being suitable for digital tracking. This meant its geometry, internal mechanism, user interaction and virtual representation all had to be considered together.

The project gave me experience working within a research environment where rapid prototyping and experimentation were used to explore possible solutions rather than immediately developing a fixed final product.

Research & Concept Development

The first stage of the project involved researching existing push-push, toggle and latching mechanisms.

I investigated how different designs changed between physical states, how springs and guide features controlled their movement, and which concepts could realistically be manufactured using desktop 3D printing.

Existing mechanism examples were studied and, where practical, reproduced through printed prototypes. This provided a clearer understanding of how small changes in geometry influenced reliability, friction and user feel.

Ideas and observations were organised using a visual research board, allowing the strongest features from several concepts to be compared before developing my own design.

Mechanical Design Development

The mechanism progressed from an early functional concept into a more compact and ergonomic cylindrical design.

The first version was created in Onshape to quickly explore the movement of the internal components. This allowed the basic mechanism to be tested before additional effort was invested in refinement.

The design was later developed further in Autodesk Fusion. The geometry, clearances, spring positions and external shape were adjusted as physical testing revealed where the mechanism could bind, rotate incorrectly or feel uncomfortable to operate.

Early mechanism design used to test the basic latching motion.

Improved cylindrical design developed for better ergonomics and more consistent operation.

A major design improvement was changing the outer body into a cylindrical form. This made the mechanism more comfortable to hold and reduced the effect that orientation and gravity had on the movement of the internal parts.

The design also needed to remain simple enough to assemble and modify. Because the project was exploratory, individual parts had to be easy to reprint and replace as dimensions and internal geometry changed.

Springs & Prototype Iteration

The spring system was critical to the operation of the mechanism. It controlled the return force, button travel and physical feel experienced by the user.

The design required a balance between spring force, friction, clearances and print quality. A spring that was too weak could not move the mechanism reliably, while a stronger spring increased friction and made the button difficult to operate.

Small dimensional changes had a large influence on performance. Clearances that appeared suitable in CAD did not always work once printed because of layer lines, surface finish and dimensional variation.

Each prototype was assembled and tested before the next version was produced. This allowed mechanical issues to be isolated and corrected through repeated CAD and printing iterations.

Printing Tolerances

Printed dimensions did not always match the CAD model closely enough for sliding components. Clearances had to be adjusted through physical testing.

Friction & Binding

Layer lines and small alignment errors could cause the mechanism to catch or bind, requiring changes to guide geometry and contact surfaces.

Interaction Feel

The button needed to operate reliably while also providing a clear and intuitive physical response to the user.

Unity development used to create the virtual environment and begin connecting it with the physical mechanism.

Unity Development

A major part of the project involved learning Unity and beginning to connect the physical prototype with a digital environment.

I began by learning the Unity interface, scene hierarchy, object relationships and basic scripting in C#.

I also worked with prefabs, animations, camera behaviour and the Meta SDK while developing a basic mixed reality environment in which the mechanism could be represented.

This part of the project required a different approach from the mechanical development. Rather than testing clearances and spring forces, I had to understand coordinate systems, object behaviour and the relationship between physical movement and a virtual representation.

Mixed Reality Tracking

Once the physical mechanism was operating reliably, the next stage was to track its position within Unity.

The goal was to align a digital version of the mechanism with the physical object so that its movement could become part of the mixed reality experience.

Tracking introduced challenges that were not present during mechanical prototyping. The system had to recognise the object, maintain alignment and represent its motion consistently while the user interacted with it.

This stage demonstrated that successful physical-digital interaction depends on both a reliable mechanical object and a stable software representation.

Results & Future Development

By the end of the project, I had researched existing latching systems, designed and manufactured a custom mechanism, developed a more ergonomic cylindrical version, learned the foundations of Unity and begun integrating the prototype with mixed reality tracking.

The project successfully demonstrated the core physical mechanism and established a pathway for connecting it with a digital environment.

Future development could include improving tracking stability, detecting the mechanism's physical button state within Unity and refining the design so the tracking features are less visually intrusive.

Further mechanical development could also explore alternative latching systems, different spring arrangements and improved manufacturing tolerances.

Key Lessons

This project broadened my understanding of mechatronics by combining mechanical prototyping with software and mixed reality interaction.

One of the largest lessons was that a mechanism cannot be developed only within CAD. Friction, print tolerances, spring behaviour and user feel all needed to be evaluated using physical prototypes.

The project also showed that physical-digital interaction requires both sides of the system to work reliably. A well-designed mechanism still needs accurate tracking and software integration before it can become an effective mixed reality interface.

Working within the HIT Lab research environment developed my experience with rapid prototyping, experimental design, CAD iteration, Unity, C# and communicating ideas across mechanical and software-focused work.

Main takeaway

The most effective solution emerged through repeated cycles of research, design, printing, testing and refinement rather than from attempting to perfect the mechanism before building it.
