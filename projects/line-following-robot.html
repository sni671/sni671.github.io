Project Overview

The objective of this project was to design and build an autonomous line-following robot capable of completing a marked course quickly and reliably.

Completed as part of a team of three, the project combined mechanical design, embedded software and electronics into a complete mechatronic system. As a group we designed both the main controller PCB and the sensor PCB in KiCad, before manufacturing, assembling and testing the complete robot.

The design evolved through three major iterations. Rather than trying to perfect the robot in CAD, each version was built and tested before identifying weaknesses and implementing improvements in the next design. This iterative approach ultimately produced a significantly lighter, faster and more reliable robot.

Electronics

Rather than using an Arduino or other development board, we designed the robot around a custom PCB.

The electronics were designed as a team using KiCad, giving us complete control over the system layout while providing valuable experience in schematic capture, PCB layout and hardware debugging.

The robot uses an ATmega328P microcontroller mounted on the main PCB, together with the motor driver circuitry, power regulation, programming header and debugging LEDs. A separate PCB houses the five reflective sensors used to detect the position of the line.

Separating the sensor board from the controller made it much easier to experiment with sensor placement throughout the design iterations without redesigning the main electronics.

Mechanical Design

The robot progressed through three major iterations, with each version addressing the shortcomings discovered during physical testing.

Mark 1

The first prototype used a laser-cut wooden chassis together with 3D-printed gears, wheels and motor mounts. This version allowed the electronics and software to be tested quickly but exposed several mechanical issues.

The sensors were positioned too far ahead of the rear axle, causing a delay between detecting the line and steering corrections. The original gear ratio also produced insufficient torque, causing the motors to stall under load.

The first working prototype used for initial testing.

Second iteration with improved layout and sensor positioning.

Mark 2

The second version retained much of the original hardware but shortened the chassis and repositioned the sensor board closer to the axle. This produced noticeably better cornering performance and faster response to changes in the line.

Although handling improved, the drivetrain still lacked sufficient torque and the chassis remained wider than desired. These observations led to a complete redesign of the robot.

Final Design

The final robot was redesigned from the ground up to reduce weight, improve manoeuvrability and eliminate the drivetrain issues discovered during testing.

A third gear was introduced between each motor and wheel, increasing the theoretical drivetrain torque by approximately 4.4×. The wooden base was replaced with a fully 3D-printed chassis incorporating integrated mounting points for the PCB, battery and motors.

The narrower wheelbase and improved weight distribution produced a much more responsive robot, while the revised drivetrain eliminated the motor stalling experienced in earlier versions.

Final CAD model of the completed robot.

Embedded Software

The robot was programmed in Embedded C using a simple but highly effective Bang-Bang controller.

Five analogue sensor readings were continuously monitored to determine the robot's position relative to the line. PWM outputs controlled the speed of the left and right motors, allowing the robot to steer by varying the speed difference between each wheel.

Considerable time was spent tuning sensor thresholds, PWM values and overall behaviour to maximise speed while maintaining reliability. Throughout development, software changes were always validated through real-world testing rather than simulation alone.

Final assembled robot following mechanical and software optimisation.

Results

Following the complete mechanical redesign and software tuning, the robot successfully met all of the project's performance requirements.

Compared with the original prototype, the final robot was lighter, more manoeuvrable and significantly more reliable. The improved drivetrain eliminated the motor stalling issues experienced during early testing, while the revised sensor placement allowed the control algorithm to respond much more effectively through corners.

315.5 g Final Weight
4.4× Drivetrain Torque Increase
<30 s Course Time
>90% Completion Rate

Rather than relying on software alone, the project's success came from continually improving every part of the system. Mechanical design, electronics and embedded software were developed together, resulting in a robot that was substantially more capable than the initial prototype.

Key Lessons

This project demonstrated the importance of treating a mechatronic system as a whole rather than optimising each subsystem independently.

The largest improvements were achieved through mechanical redesign rather than increasingly complex software. Better sensor placement, improved gearing and a lighter chassis all made the control algorithm significantly easier to tune.

Working as part of a team also provided valuable experience in collaborative engineering. Throughout the project we jointly designed the electronics in KiCad, manufactured custom PCBs, assembled the robot, tested each iteration and used the results to guide future design decisions.

Overall this project strengthened my experience in mechanical design, 3D printing, PCB development, embedded programming, hardware debugging and iterative engineering design. It also reinforced an approach that I continue to use throughout my UAV projects—build early, test often and let experimental results drive the next design iteration.

The completed robot represented the culmination of multiple design iterations, combining custom electronics, embedded software and mechanical redesign into a reliable autonomous system.
