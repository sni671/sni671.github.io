Line Following Robot

A compact autonomous robot developed through PCB design, mechanical iteration and embedded control, achieving reliable sub-30-second course completion.

View Project
Overview Electronics Mechanical Design Software Results

Project Overview

The objective of this project was to design and build an autonomous robot capable of following a marked course quickly and reliably using a custom electronic, mechanical and software system.

The project was divided into three main areas: designing the control and sensor PCBs, developing the mechanical chassis and drivetrain, and writing the embedded software used to interpret the sensors and control the motors.

The final robot had to weigh less than 400 g, use only the supplied components and complete the course in under 30 seconds with at least a 90% success rate.

Final Mass 315.5 g
Control Method Bang-Bang control
Sensor Array Five TCRT5000 optical sensors
Microcontroller ATmega328P

Electrical & PCB Design

The electrical system used two separate printed circuit boards: a main controller board and a dedicated sensor board. Separating the boards improved modularity and made testing, wiring and debugging easier.

The main PCB contained the ATmega328P microcontroller, power regulation, motor drivers, programming connections, status LEDs and sensor inputs.

The sensor board used five TCRT5000 reflective optical sensors. Using five sensors provided more information about the robot's position relative to the line and allowed the outer sensors to detect sharper turns.

  • ATmega328P microcontroller
  • Five reflective line sensors
  • PWM motor control
  • Separate power regulation
  • Modular headers and test points

Custom controller and sensor PCBs used for sensing, motor control and embedded processing.

Mechanical Design Iterations

The mechanical system progressed through three major versions. Each iteration addressed problems identified during testing, particularly sensor placement, torque, wheelbase width and chassis weight.

Mark 1 — Initial Prototype

The first version used a laser-cut wooden base with 3D-printed motor mounts, wheels and gears. It provided a quick starting point but exposed several major problems.

The sensors were positioned too far ahead of the axle, causing delayed steering response and frequent corner overshoot. The gear ratio also provided insufficient torque, causing the motors to stall at lower speeds.

Initial wooden chassis with 3D-printed drivetrain components.

Shortened second iteration with the sensors moved closer to the axle.

Mark 2 — Revised Layout

The second version reused most of the original components but shortened the base and repositioned the electronics to move the sensors closer to the axle.

This improved cornering response, but the wide wheelbase still limited agility and the unchanged gear ratio meant the motors continued to stall.

Final Design

The final version was a complete redesign focused on compactness, lower mass and increased drivetrain torque.

A third gear was added between each motor and wheel, increasing the theoretical torque by approximately 4.4 times. The wooden base was replaced by a fully 3D-printed chassis with integrated supports, cable routing and component mounts.

The narrower wheelbase improved manoeuvrability, while the centrally aligned battery, controller and sensors produced better weight distribution.

Final compact CAD design incorporating the revised gear train and integrated 3D-printed chassis.

Embedded Control Software

The robot used a Bang-Bang control approach running on the ATmega328P. The five sensors were read through the microcontroller's analogue inputs, while PWM outputs controlled the speed of the two motors.

The difference between the central sensors was used to determine which direction the robot needed to turn. The two outer sensors detected larger deviations and triggered stronger left or right corrections for tight corners.

The initial software only pulsed one motor at a time. This caused aggressive oscillation around the line and made the robot unstable when approaching tighter turns.

The control logic was improved by applying different PWM values to both motors simultaneously. This produced smoother convergence toward the line and significantly improved reliability.

Completed robot with the custom PCB, five-sensor board and final 3D-printed chassis.

Results

The early versions struggled to complete the full course consistently. The final mechanical redesign solved the major torque and handling issues, after which software tuning was used to increase speed while preserving reliability.

315.5 g Final robot mass
4.4× Approximate torque increase
<30 s Course completion time
90% Final completion reliability

The project demonstrated how closely linked mechanical design, electronics and software are in a mechatronic system. The largest performance improvement did not come from a single area, but from redesigning the drivetrain, repositioning the sensors, reducing the chassis mass and refining the control logic together.

Key Lessons

The most important lesson from this project was that software cannot fully compensate for poor mechanical design. The initial control system struggled because the sensors, wheelbase and gear ratio made the robot inherently difficult to control.

Once the mechanical system was redesigned, the software became much easier to tune and the robot's reliability improved substantially. The project also developed my experience in PCB design, embedded C, sensor integration, mechanical iteration, 3D printing and system-level debugging.
